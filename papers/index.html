  1. The Impact of Terrorism on Education: Evidence from the Death of Osama bin Laden in Pakistan
    with Chungeun Yoon
    2024
    Abstract

    This paper investigates the impact of terrorism on educational outcomes following the death of Osama bin Laden in Pakistan. The unexpected death of bin Laden, Al-Qaeda’s founder and symbolic leader, triggered a significant escalation in retaliatory terrorist attacks. Terrorist groups perceived Pakistan as complicit in the U.S. operation, leading to increased attacks on government, military, and civilian targets, with assaults on educational institutions rising by 50 percent. This surge led to a significant decline in educational attainment, reducing average years of schooling by 0.52 years and lowering primary school completion rates by 5.9 percentage points, with a more pronounced impact on girls. Furthermore, academic performance declined, with math, English, and reading scores falling by approximately 0.1 standard deviations. These findings highlight that the elimination of a terrorist leader can incite retaliatory violence, significantly undermining the educational prospects of children, particularly girls.


  2. The Impact of Terrorism on Voter Turnout: Evidence from Instrumental Variable Estimation
    2024
    Abstract

    This paper investigates the impact of exposure to terrorist attacks on voter turnout in national elections. Given the potential endogeneity in terrorist attack targeting, we construct a set of novel instruments to address this issue. Specifically, we use exogenous variation in the timing and location of major events such as the death of Osama bin Laden and the Red Mosque siege. Using these instruments, we find that terrorist attacks suppress voter turnout by 5 percent and 3 percent more than what is predicted by traditional difference-in-differences estimators. This suggests that terrorist organizations may specifically target areas with positive shocks to enhance their impact on election outcomes. Our findings highlight the role of fear and population migration as key mechanisms driving reduced voter participation, rather than any direct disruption to electoral infrastructure. This study contributes to understanding the broader political and social consequences of terrorism beyond immediate violence.


  3. Improving Displaced Livelihoods: A Training Program for Afghan Refugees in Pakistan
    with Sungho Park
    2024
    Abstract

    This randomized controlled trial (RCT) evaluates the effectiveness of an intervention aimed at improving the livelihoods of vulnerable Afghan refugee youth in Pakistan by providing them with essential vocational and digital skills. The refugee population faces significant barriers to employment, such as legal restrictions, limited access to basic services like healthcare and education, and a lack of economic opportunities. The intervention addresses these challenges by offering a series of tailored courses that focus on skills in high demand within the local labor market. These courses cover both vocational training and digital literacy, ensuring that the refugees acquire valuable capabilities that can improve their job prospects. Participants are randomly selected and are enrolled in daily training sessions conducted in groups over a three-month period. The intervention design incorporates the strategic use of technology to enhance learning and the involvement of religious leaders to motivate refugees to participate in the program. A follow-up evaluation, conducted three months after the completion of the training, will assess the long-term impacts on the participants’ employment outcomes, skills retention, and overall well-being. This evaluation is based on phone surveys, providing important insights into the sustainability and effectiveness of the intervention.


  4. Spillovers of Digitization
    2024
    Abstract

    This paper investigates the spillover effects of land record digitization, using high-frequency, incident-level data on corruption, combined with datasets on agricultural credit and land use. The digitization of land records is a key policy reform aimed at improving transparency and reducing corruption within the land administration system. By exploiting the staggered rollout of this program across different regions, the study conducts an intent-to-treat analysis and supplements it with a local average treatment effect (LATE) approach to evaluate the causal impact of digitization on corruption. The results indicate that the availability of transparent, digital land records plays a significant role in reducing corrupt practices, especially by decreasing the discretion of officials in land-related transactions. Furthermore, the analysis highlights that these improvements in transparency are more influential than efforts focused on strengthening the capacity of the state institutions. The findings offer valuable policy implications for other developing countries seeking to reduce corruption and improve governance through the digitization of public services, with broader economic and social benefits stemming from the enhanced accessibility of land-related information.


  5. The Long-Term Impact of In-Utero Exposure to Natural Disasters on Human Capital
    2024
    Abstract

    This paper explores the long-term effects of in-utero exposure to natural disasters on human capital outcomes, using the 2005 earthquake in Pakistan as a natural experiment. The earthquake, which struck unexpectedly in the fall of 2005, offers a unique opportunity to test the fetal origins hypothesis, which suggests that prenatal environmental shocks can have lasting effects on individuals’ health, education, and income. The study takes advantage of regional and cohort-level variations in exposure to the earthquake, allowing for a comparison between those born just before or after the disaster in different districts. The results show that individuals who were exposed to the earthquake in utero exhibit significantly worse health outcomes, including increased disability rates, as well as lower educational attainment and income levels later in life. These findings underscore the importance of prenatal health interventions and provide strong evidence that in-utero exposure to adverse environmental shocks can have long-lasting, negative effects on human capital development. This research has significant policy implications for improving prenatal care and disaster preparedness, particularly in regions vulnerable to natural disasters.


  6. Impact Evaluation of Billion Tree Tsunami Afforestation Project in the Province Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Pakistan
    with Inayat Ullah
    2024
    Abstract

    This study assesses the short-term and long-term impacts of the Billion Tree Tsunami Afforestation Project (BTTAP) in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa (KP), Pakistan, which was launched in 2013 as part of the province’s efforts to combat climate change. The project aims to enhance forest cover, conserve biodiversity, and reduce the impacts of environmental degradation. In line with the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), the BTTAP has focused on promoting sustainable environmental practices, improving employment opportunities, and raising awareness on climate-related issues. We evaluate its effects on key areas including employment, public health, environmental quality, pollution levels, internal migration patterns, and animal habitats. To measure these outcomes, we collect data from the general public, project stakeholders, and government officials, supplemented by the Pakistan Social Living Standard Measurement (PSLM) dataset, which provides comprehensive information on health and employment indicators. Utilizing GIS mapping techniques, we identify treatment areas and apply difference-in-differences and instrumental variable approaches with fixed effects to rigorously analyze the data. Although the evaluation is still in its early stages, our findings aim to contribute valuable insights into the effectiveness of the BTTAP in advancing environmental sustainability and improving the livelihoods of communities in KP. This study provides essential information for policymakers and stakeholders working towards achieving the SDGs and fostering long-term sustainable development in the region.


  7. Barriers to Education: Parental Choices and Child Labor Dynamics Among Afghan Refugees in Pakistan
    with Inayat UllahSaeeda Khanum
    2024
    Abstract

    Understanding the factors behind out-of-school Afghan migrant’s children and their increased child labor in Pakistan is crucially important. This study was carried out to identify factors that affect parents’ decision-making process in the context of child-labor and child-schooling. In a sequential mixed method study design interviews with Afghan refugee parents were conducted to explore their perspective of education and later based on those findings a quantitative survey was conducted to identify prevalence of school-aged children vulnerable to child labor and hazardous work. This study enabled us to observe the living conditions of vulnerable refugee households, including their substandard shelters and poor hygiene, within the slum settlements located in the proximity of the capital city, as well as in the refugee camps situated in the Haripur district of the Khyber Pakhtunkhwa province, Pakistan. Results revealed dependence on child income increases the ratio of out of school children by 10 percent (p<0.01), increase the probability of child labor within household by nearly 0.22 percentage points (p<0.001) and preference of child labor upon education is increased by 3.8-fold (p<0.001).
